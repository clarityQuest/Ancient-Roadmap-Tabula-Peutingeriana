Tabula Peutingeriana

The Road Map of the Ancient World

The Tabula Peutingeriana is one of the most remarkable surviving documents of antiquity — a medieval copy of a Roman road map that charts the entire known world, from the Atlantic coast of Britain to the Indian subcontinent, in extraordinary detail.

A Map Unlike Any Other

This is not a geographic map in the modern sense. The scroll format forced the cartographer to compress the north-south dimension dramatically — the Mediterranean Sea appears as a narrow strip, and Italy is rotated almost horizontally. What matters is connectivity: roads, distances in Roman miles (milia passuum), and the cities they link.

Three cities receive special pictorial treatment — Rome, Constantinople, and Antioch — each shown as an enthroned figure, reflecting their supreme importance in the late Roman world.

The Lost Segment

Segment I — covering Britain (except the southeast), the Iberian Peninsula, and the Atlantic coast of Morocco — has been lost since at least the 16th century. The remaining eleven segments survive intact, making this viewer's collection complete for Segments II through XII.

History of the Document

The map was copied around 1200 AD by a monk in Colmar (Alsace), likely from an earlier Carolingian copy of a late antique original. Konrad Celtes discovered it in 1494 and passed it to Konrad Peutinger of Augsburg, who gave it its modern name. After Peutinger's death it passed through various hands before entering the Imperial Library in Vienna in 1738, where it remains today.

Latin Inscriptions — AI Translated

The Tabula is rich with Latin inscriptions: region labels, river names, curiosities like "In his locis scorpiones nascuntur" (In these places scorpions are born) or the famous "Hic Alexander responsum accepit: usque quo Alexander?" (Here Alexander received the answer: How far, Alexander?). Over 1,100 multi-word Latin place names are translated into English and German by Claude AI, visible in the place tooltip and info panel.
